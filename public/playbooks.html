911 Emergency

Possible Stroke (FAST Check)

  1. Face — Ask them to smile. Is one side drooping?
  2. Arms — Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift down?
  3. Speech — Ask them to repeat a phrase. Is it slurred or strange?
  4. Time — If ANY of these are yes, call 911. Note the exact time symptoms started.
  5. Do not give food, drink, or extra medication.
  6. Bring medication list, allergy list, insurance card, and ID to the hospital.
Time is critical — every minute without treatment can mean brain damage. The ER needs to know exact symptom onset time.
911 Emergency

Chest Pain / Heart Emergency

  1. Call 911 immediately — do not drive to the hospital.
  2. Have the patient sit or lie down in a comfortable position.
  3. If they take nitroglycerin, help them take it as prescribed.
  4. If they have aspirin and aren't allergic, have them chew one regular aspirin (325mg).
  5. Unlock the front door for EMS.
  6. Gather current medications, insurance card, and ID.
Do NOT let the patient talk you out of calling 911. Heart attacks often present with denial.
911 Emergency

Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

  1. Call 911 immediately.
  2. Use epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available — inject into outer thigh.
  3. Have the patient lie on their back with legs elevated (unless breathing difficulty — then sit upright).
  4. Remove the allergen if possible (stop eating the food, remove the bee stinger).
  5. Note what triggered the reaction and when symptoms started.
Known allergens for this patient are in the profile. Check before giving any new medication or food.
Urgent — same day
Urgent

Fall / Possible Fracture

  1. Do not force them to stand if fracture is possible.
  2. Check for head injury: confusion, vomiting, unequal pupils → call 911.
  3. Apply ice to swelling, keep the injured area still.
  4. If stable, call primary doctor for same-day guidance.
  5. Write down what happened, when, where, and who witnessed it.
  6. Take photos of any visible injuries.
Urgent

Medication Reaction

  1. If trouble breathing, facial swelling, or collapse → call 911.
  2. Stop the suspected medication (unless it's a critical med — check with doctor first).
  3. Take a photo of any visible rash or swelling.
  4. Note the medication name, dose taken, and time of the reaction.
  5. Call the prescribing doctor and pharmacy.
  6. Keep the medication bottle available for reference.
If stopping a critical medication (blood thinner, seizure med, heart medication), call the doctor BEFORE stopping.
Urgent

Blood Sugar Emergency (Diabetic)

  1. Low blood sugar (shaking, sweating, confusion): Give 15g fast sugar — 4 glucose tablets, 4oz juice, or 1 tbsp honey. Recheck in 15 minutes.
  2. High blood sugar (excessive thirst, frequent urination, fruity breath): Call doctor. If vomiting or confused → call 911.
  3. Check blood sugar if a meter is available and record the reading.
  4. Note what they ate, when they last took medication, and any unusual activity.
Administrative — take action today
Same Day

Medication Running Out

  1. Call the pharmacy — confirm how many refills remain.
  2. If no refills, call the prescribing doctor's office and request an urgent refill.
  3. Ask for the authorization staff if prior auth is needed.
  4. Write down every person's name and the time you called.
  5. If the medication will run out before the refill arrives, ask the pharmacy about emergency partial fills.
Same Day

Insurance Denial Received

  1. Read the denial letter — note the denial reason and deadline to appeal.
  2. Call the insurance company's member services number on the letter.
  3. Ask for the clinical criteria they used to deny the claim.
  4. Call the prescribing/ordering doctor and ask them to submit a peer-to-peer review.
  5. File a formal appeal before the deadline — use Health Agent to draft the letter.
Most denials can be overturned on appeal. The key is acting fast and getting the doctor involved.
